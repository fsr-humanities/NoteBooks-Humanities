# Chapter 1: Constitution: Why and How? — Glossary

**Subject:** Indian Constitution at Work · Class XI Political Science (NCERT)

> Terms sorted A → Z. Constitutional provisions in backticks where applicable. ★ = high exam frequency.

---

### A

**Allegiance**
Voluntary loyalty and fidelity given by citizens to their constitution and the political community it creates; distinguished from mere compliance under coercion in that it reflects genuine acceptance of the constitutional framework as legitimate. A constitution commands allegiance when it gives every group — including minorities — some reason to go along with its provisions. *Context in this chapter:* Used to explain why constitutions that systematically privilege some groups at the expense of others lose the allegiance of disadvantaged groups and become ineffective.

**Aspirations and goals of a society**
The positive social, economic, and political objectives that a society wishes to achieve over time — such as eliminating caste or racial discrimination, ensuring minimum material well-being, and providing education — which a constitution may enshrine and empower the government to pursue. *Context in this chapter:* Identified as the subject of the fourth function of a constitution; the Indian Constitution is cited as the finest example of a 20th-century constitution with an enabling framework for such aspirations.

**Authority of a constitution** ★
The legitimate power of a constitutional document to bind both citizens and government to its provisions; it derives from three factors: the public credibility of those who enacted it, the fairness of its substantive provisions in giving everyone a reason to go along, and the intelligence of its institutional design in preventing subversion. *Context in this chapter:* The entire second section of the chapter is devoted to explaining what makes constitutional authority real rather than merely formal.

---

### B

**Balanced institutional design** ★
A constitutional arrangement in which governmental power is intelligently distributed across multiple institutions — legislature, executive, judiciary, and independent statutory bodies — such that no single institution can acquire monopoly power and each can check the others' transgressions; also requires striking the right balance between constitutional rigidity (to protect core values) and flexibility (to allow adaptation to changing circumstances). *Context in this chapter:* Identified as the third factor for constitutional effectiveness; the Indian Constitution's horizontal fragmentation of power across four institutions is the primary example given.

---

### C

**Cabinet Mission** ★
A committee of the British cabinet sent to India in 1946 to facilitate the transfer of power and to plan the composition of a Constituent Assembly; its plan allotted seats proportional to population at a ratio of approximately 1 seat per 10 lakh people, distributed seats among the three main communities (Muslims, Sikhs, and general), and specified proportional representation with single transferable vote as the election method. *Context in this chapter:* The Constituent Assembly was composed roughly along the lines of the Cabinet Mission plan, with Provinces electing 292 members and Princely States allotted a minimum of 93 seats.

**Checks and balances** ★
An institutional mechanism in which different branches of government — legislative, executive, judicial — and independent statutory bodies are each given powers to oversee and limit the others, preventing any single institution from exercising unchecked authority and thereby protecting constitutional governance from subversion by powerful groups. *Context in this chapter:* Described as an "intelligent system" that has "facilitated the success of the Indian Constitution"; the Legislature, Executive, Judiciary, and Election Commission are cited as the four institutions that check each other.

**Citizenship**
The legal status conferring membership in a political community, typically entailing both rights (fundamental freedoms, voting, equality before law) and obligations (compliance with the law); the Indian Constitution deliberately defines citizenship in civic terms — not based on race, caste, religion, or ethnicity — in contrast to constitutions like Germany's that historically tied citizenship to ethnic identity. *Context in this chapter:* Discussed under the fifth function — fundamental identity of a people — and in the context of Partition, where the CA committed to a conception of citizenship where religious identity would have no bearing.

**Collective identity**
The shared sense of political and moral membership that citizens of a country form by agreeing to a common set of constitutional norms and values; a constitution creates this collective identity — transforming diverse individuals into a single political people — in addition to the pre-existing identities (religious, regional, linguistic) that individuals bring to it. *Context in this chapter:* Discussed as the fifth function of a constitution; contrasted with German ethnic identity to illustrate that India's collective identity is civic rather than ethnic.

**Constituent Assembly** ★
A body of representatives convened specifically to draft a constitution; India's Constituent Assembly held its first sitting on [9 December 1946], was reconstituted for divided India on [14 August 1947], had 299 members after Partition (reduced from the original number as Pakistan-territory members left), adopted the Constitution on [26 November 1949], and saw 284 members sign it on [24 January 1950]. *Context in this chapter:* The third section of the chapter is entirely devoted to explaining how the CA was formed, how it worked, and why its processes gave the Constitution its authority.

**Constitution** ★
A body of fundamental principles or a document (or set of documents) according to which a state is constituted and governed; it performs five functions — providing basic rules for coordination, specifying decision-making power, limiting government, enabling aspirations, and expressing collective identity — and derives its authority from the credibility of its framers, the fairness of its provisions, and the intelligence of its institutional design. *Context in this chapter:* The central subject of the entire chapter; defined both abstractly (as fundamental principles) and concretely (as a document or set of documents).

**Coordination**
The first function of a constitution; the achievement of a minimal degree of cooperative social living among diverse members of society by establishing publicly known and enforceable basic rules that reduce insecurity and enable people to know what others may and may not do. *Context in this chapter:* Introduced through the thought experiment of a diverse group that needs basic rules to live together peacefully; the enforceability component is emphasised as essential because without it citizens have no reason to follow the rules.

**Cosmopolitan identity**
A conception of national identity that is open, inclusive, and not tied to narrow ethnic, religious, or regional characteristics; the Indian Constitution's cosmopolitan identity is expressed in citizenship criteria that are not based on race, caste, or religion, and in the commitment to world peace and welfare of mankind in the Objectives Resolution. *Context in this chapter:* Listed as one of the five fundamental commitments to which the Constitution gave institutional expression, drawing on the Objectives Resolution.

---

### D

**Deliberation** ★
The process of collective decision-making through careful, reasoned discussion in which participants consider the merits of different positions and give principled justifications for their views, rather than merely asserting their interests; the Constituent Assembly is presented as a model of deliberative constitution-making through public reason. *Context in this chapter:* Identified as the principle that gave the Indian CA its authority beyond mere representativeness; every clause of the Constitution was subjected to scrutiny and debate, and every argument was responded to in writing.

**Democracy — political vs social** ★
Political democracy refers to a system of government based on free elections, representative institutions, and civil liberties; social democracy — as defined by Ambedkar — is a deeper "way of life" that recognises liberty, equality, and fraternity as inseparable principles of life, without which political democracy cannot endure. *Context in this chapter:* Presented in Ambedkar's speech of [25 November 1949] as the necessary foundation: political democracy resting on social democracy — the two are inseparable.

**Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP)**
Constitutional guidelines given to the legislature and the executive as directions for governance, intended to secure social and economic justice; borrowed by India from the  **Irish Constitution** ; though generally not directly enforceable in courts, they guide the state in making laws and policies, and represent the fourth function of a constitution — enabling the government to fulfil the aspirations of a society. *Context in this chapter:* Mentioned alongside the Preamble and Fundamental Rights as part of the enabling framework of the Indian Constitution, and identified as a provision borrowed from Ireland.

---

### E

**Election Commission**
An independent statutory body established by the Indian Constitution to superintend, direct, and control the preparation of electoral rolls and the conduct of elections; cited as an example of how the Indian Constitution fragments power horizontally — not just across the three branches of government but also through independent bodies that check the transgressions of the legislature, executive, and judiciary. *Context in this chapter:* Cited in the discussion of balanced institutional design as one of four key institutions that together form India's system of checks and balances.

**Enabling framework**
A constitutional structure that not only limits what the government may do but also empowers and directs it to take positive measures — such as reducing inequalities, providing education, ensuring housing and health care — to achieve the aspirations of society; distinguished from older constitutions that merely constrained government from doing harm. *Context in this chapter:* The Indian Constitution is described as the finest example of a 20th-century constitution with such an enabling framework; South Africa and Indonesia are cited as additional examples.

**Equality**
Both formal equality (equal treatment before the law, without discrimination based on religion, caste, race, sex, or place of birth) and substantive equality (ensuring equal status and opportunities, and reducing inequalities in income and wealth); along with liberty and fraternity, one of the three inseparable principles forming what Ambedkar called the "union of trinity" of social democracy. *Context in this chapter:* Appears both in the discussion of the fifth function (collective identity), in Ambedkar's speech, and in the Objectives Resolution as one of the guarantees to all people of India.

---

### F

**Federal arrangement**
A constitutional system in which governmental powers are formally distributed between two levels — the central government and the states — each exercising defined powers within its own sphere; India adopted a quasi-federal form borrowed from Canada, characterised by a federal structure with a strong central government and residual powers vested in the Centre. *Context in this chapter:* Identified in Section 2.4.5 as one of the two key institutional choices (along with the parliamentary form) made by the CA after extensive deliberation; borrowed from Canada.

**First Past the Post**
An electoral system in which the candidate who wins the most votes in a constituency wins that seat, regardless of whether they secure an absolute majority; borrowed by India from the  **British Constitution** . *Context in this chapter:* Listed in the table of provisions borrowed from other constitutions in Section 2.4.5.

**Fraternity**
The spirit of brotherhood — mutual respect, solidarity, and concern for fellow citizens — that Ambedkar identified as the essential glue binding liberty and equality together; without fraternity, liberty and equality would remain abstract rather than becoming natural features of everyday life. *Context in this chapter:* Presented in Ambedkar's speech as the third and indispensable element of the "union of trinity" (alongside liberty and equality), and as a principle borrowed from the French constitutional tradition.

**Fundamental identity**
The political and moral selfhood that citizens acquire through the shared constitutional norms they agree to live by; a constitution gives citizens both a political identity (by defining the community of governance) and a moral identity (by defining the values they commit to uphold). *Context in this chapter:* Discussed as the fifth function of a constitution in Section 1.5; illustrated by contrasting India's civic conception of identity with Germany's historically ethnic one.

**Fundamental Rights** ★
Constitutionally guaranteed basic rights of citizens that the government is categorically prohibited from violating, typically including freedoms of speech, conscience, association, and trade, and protection from arbitrary arrest; in the Indian Constitution they embody the third function (limits on government) and are part of the enabling framework of the fourth function. *Context in this chapter:* Referenced as the primary mechanism of the third function of a constitution, and as part of the enabling framework alongside the Preamble and DPSP; the charter of Fundamental Rights is noted as borrowed from the United States Constitution.

---

### G

**Government of India Act, 1935**
A British parliamentary act that established the framework of provincial governance in British India, including the Provincial Legislative Assemblies; these assemblies became the electoral college from which members of the Constituent Assembly were indirectly chosen. *Context in this chapter:* Cited in Section 2.4 as the legal basis for the Provincial Legislative Assemblies that elected Constituent Assembly members under the Cabinet Mission's indirect election plan.

---

### I

**Indirect election**
A form of election in which citizens do not vote directly for a particular candidate or body but instead elect representatives who in turn elect a higher body; Constituent Assembly members were chosen by indirect election — by the members of the Provincial Legislative Assemblies, not by the general population. *Context in this chapter:* The chapter uses this fact to address the argument that the CA was not representative, noting that while it was not elected by universal suffrage, serious attempts at representativeness were made through other means.

---

### J

**Judicial Review**
The power of courts to examine legislation and executive actions for conformity with the constitution and to strike down those that violate constitutional provisions; borrowed by India from the **United States Constitution** along with the principle of judicial independence. *Context in this chapter:* Listed in Section 2.4.5 as one of the provisions adapted from the US Constitution; supports the system of checks and balances by enabling the judiciary to check the legislature and executive.

---

### L

**Liberty**
Freedom from arbitrary restrictions on thought, expression, belief, and action; one of the three inseparable principles of Ambedkar's social democracy — "liberty cannot be divorced from equality" because without equality, liberty would produce the supremacy of the few over the many. *Context in this chapter:* Appears in Ambedkar's speech, in the Objectives Resolution (fundamental freedoms guarantee), and as a principle adapted from the French constitutional tradition.

**Living document**
A constitution that is capable of evolving through amendment and judicial interpretation to meet changing needs and circumstances, while preserving its core values; the Indian Constitution is described as a living document because it balances the possibility of amendment with limits on those amendments, ensuring neither dangerous rigidity nor dangerous flexibility. *Context in this chapter:* Mentioned in Section 2.3 in the context of balanced institutional design; the chapter notes that Chapter 9 of the textbook elaborates on this concept.

---

### M

**Mode of promulgation** ★
The manner in which a constitution comes into existence — specifically, whether it was created by credible figures enjoying popular trust and whether the process of drafting reflected broad participation and consensus; identified as the first of three factors determining a constitution's effectiveness. *Context in this chapter:* Section 2.1 uses the contrast between Indian constitution-making (emerging from a popular nationalist movement with credible leaders) and cases like Nepal (constitutions 'granted' by a king) to explain why mode of promulgation matters.

**Monarchical constitution**
A constitutional arrangement in which sovereign power is vested in a hereditary monarch who alone has the authority to decide what the laws will be; cited as one of the ways different constitutional systems answer the question of "who gets to decide." *Context in this chapter:* Mentioned in Section 1.2 as a contrast to democratic constitutions, in the discussion of how different systems specify decision-making power differently.

**Moral identity**
The ethical selfhood that citizens acquire through a constitution — by committing to the fundamental values (liberty, equality, fraternity, justice) that the constitution upholds as inviolable; distinct from but complementary to political identity, which is about membership in a political community. *Context in this chapter:* Discussed in Section 1.5 as the second dimension of the fifth function (collective identity); a constitution defines the fundamental values that citizens may not trespass, thereby giving them a moral identity.

---

### N

**Nationalist movement**
A broad political and social movement seeking independence from colonial rule and the establishment of a self-governing nation-state; India's nationalist movement, spanning decades of struggle under leaders like Gandhi, Nehru, Patel, and Ambedkar, forged the consensus on values and principles (equality, liberty, democracy, sovereignty, cosmopolitan identity) that the Constituent Assembly translated into constitutional provisions. *Context in this chapter:* Identified in Section 2.4.4 as the source from which the CA inherited its guiding principles; the Objectives Resolution is described as the best summary of those principles.

---

### O

**Objectives Resolution** ★
A resolution moved by Jawaharlal Nehru in 1946 that defined the aims of the Constituent Assembly; it declared India an independent sovereign republic, guaranteed social, economic and political justice, fundamental freedoms, equality before law, and provided safeguards for minorities and backward classes; it is described as the "best summary" of the principles the nationalist movement brought to constitution-making, and the Indian Constitution gave these principles institutional form. *Context in this chapter:* Discussed in Section 2.4.4 as the moral foundation of the Indian Constitution; the chapter reproduces all 8 main points of the resolution in a coloured callout box.

---

### P

**Parliamentary form of government**
A system of government in which the executive (the Council of Ministers, led by the Prime Minister) is drawn from and remains accountable to the legislature (Parliament); the Parliament itself may remove the executive through a vote of no confidence; borrowed by India from the  **British Constitution** . *Context in this chapter:* Identified in Section 2.4.5 as one of the two key institutional choices made by the CA; noted alongside the federal arrangement as part of the balanced institutional design.

**Political identity**
The sense of belonging to a specific political community — a nation, state, or polity — that citizens acquire by agreeing to a shared set of constitutional norms; the first dimension of the fifth function of a constitution. *Context in this chapter:* Introduced in Section 1.5 to explain how a constitution creates a collective "we" out of diverse individuals who agree on basic norms of governance.

**Preamble**
The introductory statement of the Indian Constitution that declares the source of constitutional authority (the people of India), the nature of the state (sovereign, socialist, secular, democratic republic), and the fundamental goals (justice, liberty, equality, fraternity); it provides the foundation for the enabling provisions of the Constitution. *Context in this chapter:* Mentioned in Section 1.4 as providing support for the enabling provisions of the Constitution alongside Fundamental Rights and DPSP.

**Princely States**
Semi-autonomous territories in British India that were under the sovereignty of local rulers (princes or maharajas) rather than under direct British administration; under the Cabinet Mission plan they were allotted a minimum of 93 seats in the Constituent Assembly, with the method of selecting their representatives to be determined by consultation. *Context in this chapter:* Discussed in Section 2.4 as the second category of entities (alongside Provinces) that were represented in the Constituent Assembly under the Cabinet Mission plan.

**Promulgation**
The formal act of publicly announcing and putting into effect a law or constitutional document; in the context of constitutions, it refers specifically to the manner and authority by which a constitution is brought into existence and declared binding. *Context in this chapter:* The term "mode of promulgation" is used in Section 2.1 to refer to the entire process by which a constitution comes into being — including who crafted it and how much authority those persons had.

**Proportional representation**
An electoral system in which the share of seats a community or party receives in a representative body corresponds to their share of the electorate; used in the Constituent Assembly elections, where members of each community (Muslim, Sikh, general) in the Provincial Legislative Assemblies elected their own CA representatives in proportion to their numbers, using single transferable vote. *Context in this chapter:* Described in Section 2.4 as the election method used for community-wise selection of CA members under the Cabinet Mission plan.

**Provincial Legislative Assembly**
An elected legislature at the level of a province under the Government of India Act, 1935; these bodies served as the electoral college for the indirect election of Constituent Assembly members — CA members were chosen by provincial assembly members, not directly by the general public. *Context in this chapter:* Cited in Section 2.4 as the institutional mechanism through which Constituent Assembly members were indirectly elected.

**Public reason** ★
The practice of making political decisions through open discussion in which participants offer principled justifications — reasons that can be assessed by any member of the community — rather than merely asserting self-interest or exercising raw power; the Constituent Assembly of India is cited as a prime historical example of public reason in constitution-making. *Context in this chapter:* Introduced in Section 2.4.2 to explain the deeper source of the CA's authority — not just its representativeness, but the quality of its deliberative process, visible to the press and public, in which every argument was answered in writing.

---

### Q

**Quasi-federal form of government**
A constitutional arrangement that combines features of both federal and unitary systems — distributing power between a central government and constituent states (federal), while simultaneously giving the central government significantly stronger powers than in a pure federation; borrowed by India from the  **Canadian Constitution** , along with the idea of residual powers vesting in the Centre. *Context in this chapter:* Identified in Section 2.4.5 as India's form of government borrowed from Canada, characterised as "a federal system with a strong central government."

---

### R

**Referendum**
A direct vote in which all eligible citizens vote on a specific question — such as whether to approve a constitution — rather than acting through elected representatives; India never subjected its Constitution to a referendum, yet the document carried enormous public authority because it had the consensus and backing of leaders who were themselves popular. *Context in this chapter:* Discussed in Section 2.1 to explain that democratic legitimacy for a constitution does not require a referendum; what matters is the public credibility of the framers and whether people adopt the constitution as their own.

**Residual Powers**
Constitutional powers over subjects not explicitly enumerated in any list assigned to the central government or the states — residual powers fill the gaps left when the constitution's enumerated lists do not cover a particular subject; India borrowed the concept from the  **Canadian Constitution** , vesting residual powers in the central government (unlike the USA, where residual powers vest in the states). *Context in this chapter:* Listed in Section 2.4.5 as one of the two provisions adapted from the Canadian Constitution.

**Rule of Law**
The principle that all persons and institutions — including the government itself — are subject to and accountable to the law; no one is above the law, and the law must be applied equally and fairly; borrowed by India from the  **British constitutional tradition** . *Context in this chapter:* Listed in Section 2.4.5 as one of the provisions adapted from the British Constitution; it supports the third function of a constitution (limits on government power) by ensuring government actions are constrained by law.

---

### S

**Single transferable vote**
A form of proportional representation voting in which voters rank candidates in order of preference, and votes are redistributed from eliminated candidates to remaining ones according to voters' stated preferences, until seats are filled; used for the election of Constituent Assembly members from each community within the Provincial Legislative Assemblies. *Context in this chapter:* Described in Section 2.4 as the specific voting method used under the Cabinet Mission plan for the community-wise election of CA members.

**Social democracy**
As defined by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar: a way of life that recognises liberty, equality, and fraternity as inseparable principles forming a "union of trinity" — where none can be divorced from the others without defeating the purpose of democracy; presented as the deeper foundation that political democracy must rest upon in order to endure. *Context in this chapter:* Articulated in Ambedkar's famous speech to the CA on [25 November 1949]; the chapter presents this as the broader vision underlying the constitutional framework.

**Sovereignty**
The supreme and independent authority of a state over its own affairs, free from external control; declared in the very first point of the Objectives Resolution ("India is an independent, sovereign, republic") and one of the five fundamental commitments to which the Constitution gave institutional expression. *Context in this chapter:* Mentioned in Section 2.4.4 as one of the core principles derived from the Objectives Resolution and embedded in the Constitution.

**Substantive provisions**
The actual content of a constitution — the specific rights it protects, the institutions it creates, the powers it distributes, and the values it upholds — as distinct from the procedural aspects of how it was created; identified as the second factor for constitutional effectiveness; a constitution whose substantive provisions give everyone a reason to go along with them is more likely to succeed. *Context in this chapter:* Discussed in Section 2.2; the point is made that a constitution that systematically disadvantages any group will cease to command that group's allegiance.

---

### U

**Universal suffrage** ★
The principle and practice that all adult citizens have the right to vote, regardless of religion, caste, education, gender, or income; in the Indian Constitution context, it was the one provision that was passed by the Constituent Assembly without virtually any debate — so self-evidently just and correct was it considered by all members. *Context in this chapter:* Discussed in Section 2.4.2 as the unique example of undebated consensus in the CA; contrasted with every other provision of the Constitution, which was seriously discussed and argued over.

## ⚠️ Easily Confused Pairs

> [!warning] Adoption Date vs Commencement Date
> **Adopted [26 November 1949]:** The date on which the Constituent Assembly formally accepted the Constitution as complete; now observed as Constitution Day (Samvidhan Divas).
> **Came into force [26 January 1950]:** The date on which the Constitution became operative as the supreme law of India; observed as Republic Day. The 284-member signing ceremony was [24 January 1950] — a third distinct date.
> *Exam trap:* Questions frequently ask specifically for one of these dates; using the wrong one costs a full mark.

> [!warning] Social Democracy vs Political Democracy
> **Political democracy:** A system of government based on elections, representative institutions, and civil liberties — the procedural dimension of democracy.
> **Social democracy (Ambedkar):** A way of life recognising liberty, equality, and fraternity as inseparable principles — the substantive cultural dimension of democracy.
> *Exam trap:* Questions ask students to distinguish the two; the key point is that Ambedkar argued political democracy CANNOT survive without social democracy at its foundation.

> [!warning] Fundamental Rights vs Directive Principles of State Policy
> **Fundamental Rights:** Justiciable rights guaranteed to every citizen; enforceable in courts; government is prohibited from violating them; borrowed from the United States Constitution.
> **Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP):** Guidelines for governance directing the state toward social and economic justice; generally not directly enforceable in courts; represent the government's obligations rather than citizens' rights; borrowed from the Irish Constitution.
> *Exam trap:* Questions ask students to distinguish or compare the two; the key distinction is justiciability (courts can enforce Fundamental Rights but not DPSP directly) and the source country of borrowing.

> [!warning] Mode of Promulgation vs Substantive Provisions
> **Mode of promulgation:** HOW a constitution came into being — the credibility of its framers, the process of its creation, whether it emerged from popular participation.
> **Substantive provisions:** WHAT the constitution actually says — the rights it guarantees, the powers it distributes, the values it upholds.
> *Exam trap:* Both are among the three factors for constitutional effectiveness; students confuse them or treat them as the same thing; the distinction is process (promulgation) vs content (substantive provisions).
